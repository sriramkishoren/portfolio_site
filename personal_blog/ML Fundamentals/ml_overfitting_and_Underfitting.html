The Dartboard Analogy: Bias vs. Variance

Shooting darts at a target is a classic analogy to explain the relationship between bias (systematic error) and variance (sensitivity).

High Bias / Low Variance

UNDERFITTING

"Darts are tight together, but nowhere near the bullseye."

Systematically off-target. Model is too simple.

Low Bias / High Variance

OVERFITTING

"Darts are all over the place, but centered around the bullseye."

Sensitive to fluctuations. Memorizes noise.

Low Bias / Low Variance

OPTIMAL FIT

"All darts land on the bullseye."

Captures patterns, ignores noise.

Underfitting

High Bias

What is it?

The model is too straightforward and lacks complexity. It struggles to capture nuanced relationships within the data. It is "systematically wrong."

Typical Candidates

  • Linear Regression (too simple for curves)
  • Logistic Regression (simple classification)

Symptoms

  • Poor performance on Training data
  • Poor performance on Test data
  • Fails to explain variance (High Bias)

Overfitting

High Variance

What is it?

The model becomes excessively intricate. It learns the training data "too well," including the noise, preventing it from generalizing to new, unseen data.

Typical Candidates

  • Random Forests (if unchecked)
  • Neural Networks (Deep Learning)
  • XGBoost

Symptoms

  • Great performance on Training data
  • Poor performance on Test data
  • Sensitive to small changes (High Variance)

Diagnosis: Reading Learning Curves

Measuring the trade-off between overfitting and underfitting can be difficult, but monitoring error rates is key.

What is Error/Loss?

"Loss" quantifies how far a model's predictions are from the actual values. For example, in predicting house prices, this might be the Mean Squared Error (average squared difference between predicted and actual price). Lower is always better.

Solutions & Remedies

How to Fix Underfitting

  • Increase Model Complexity

    Switch from simple linear models to models that can capture curves and nuances.

  • Add More Parameters/Features

    Consider more variables (e.g., neighborhood, remodel date) to capture the phenomenon.

How to Fix Overfitting

  • Get More High-Quality Data

    Ensure the complexity of the model is matched by the quantity of data.

  • Cross-Fold Validation

    Withhold data, train, and test with random fluctuations to measure variance.

  • Reduce Complexity

    Don't use a Neural Network when a simpler model generalizes better.

Key Takeaway

"A model that is too simple won't detect fine patterns, while a model that is too complex will overcompensate... ideally, what we want is an equal matchup."
