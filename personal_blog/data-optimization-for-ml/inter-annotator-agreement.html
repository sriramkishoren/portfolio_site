Inter-Annotator Agreement

Measuring and Managing Annotation Consistency for Reliable Machine Learning

Based on the lecture by Dr. Ian McCullough, Johns Hopkins University

"As a manager of a project, you should expect your teams to be assessing the consistency of their training data. Ask them for it. Ask them what measure they're using... These are all decisions that are ultimately going to help you have a better performing AI solution."

The 6 Sources of Error & Variance

Why do annotations fail? Understanding the specific type of error is the first step to fixing your data quality.

1. Recognition Errors

Annotators miss items they were supposed to identify.

Example: Missing a few cars in an image that should have been labeled.

2. Misclassification

Annotators find the item but assign the wrong label.

Example: Identifying a car but labeling it as a "dog".
Note: This is a much worse problem than recognition errors.

3. Low Data by Class

Certain classes are rare and may be omitted from the data.

Impact: Rare instances occur too infrequently for annotators to learn or models to train effectively.

4. Scale Complexity

Too many categories or too much detail exceeds cognitive capacity.

Truck vs. Car (Easy)
Honda vs. Toyota (Harder)
2021 Civic vs. 2022 Civic (Very Hard)

5. Within-Rater

An individual disagreeing with themselves over time.

Example: Annotating a document in the morning vs. the afternoon and getting different results due to fatigue or mood.

6. Between-Rater

Two different people independently assessing content and disagreeing.

Goal: This is the core of Inter-Annotator Agreement. Independent assessment is key—not focus groups.

Sloppy Annotation: Can happen across all types. Example: Drawing a mask around a person that includes background pixels or misses parts of the person. This is often an issue of care/effort rather than cognitive understanding.

Annotator Vitality

A mature diagnostic measure to identify who is helping or hurting consensus.

The Formula

Vitality = Kα − Kα(without annotator)
  • Positive Vitality: Removing this person makes consensus worse. They are contributing to quality.
  • Low/Negative Vitality: Removing this person makes consensus better. They are a source of error.

Diagnostic Flowchart

Use vitality to diagnose the specific problem with a low-performing annotator:

Scenario A Poor Recognition (Missing Items)

Annotator misses items entirely.

Action: Retrain on identification. Data might still be usable.
Scenario B (Worse) Misclassification (Wrong Labels)

Annotator finds items but labels them wrong.

Action: Throw data away or complete re-review. Remove from project.

Why Percent Agreement Fails

  • Chance Agreement: Does not account for random guessing. If people guess, agreement is not zero.
  • Multi-Category Confusion: Fails when different annotators pick different wrong categories (A vs B vs C).
  • Limited Scope: Really only works for 2 annotators.

Krippendorff's Alpha (α)

α = 1 − DobservedDexpected
α = 1.0 Perfect Agreement
α ≥ 0.80 Good Reliability (Standard)
0.67 ≤ α < 0.80 Tentative / Caution
α < 0.67 Inadequate / Discard Data
α = 0.0 Random Chance
α < 0.0 Systematic Disagreement
Handles missing data & variable annotators.

CRITICAL WARNING: The F-Measure Trap

"F-measure is NOT a measure of inter-annotator agreement. It is not accurate. It is wrong."

F-Measure

Measures Classifier Performance (Precision & Recall). Assumes one ground truth and one prediction.

IAA Metrics

Measure Consistency between peers. Assumes no single annotator holds the absolute truth.

Worked Example: The Complexity of Accuracy

Why simple accuracy fails with multiple coders and missing data.

The Scenario

  • 3 Coders: A, B, and C
  • 15 Items: Documents to categorize
  • 4 Labels: Categories 1, 2, 3, or 4
  • Constraint: Lots of missing data (not everyone rated every item)

Pairwise Calculations

A & B 3 common items, 2 agree 67% Agreement
A & C 8 common items, 6 agree 75% Agreement
B & C 5 common items, 4 agree 80% Agreement

The Aggregation Problem

"Which is the correct accuracy score?"

Do we average them? Do we weight them by how many items they shared?

Weighted Average Result 75% (But this is arbitrary!)

This ambiguity is why simple accuracy/percent agreement is insufficient for professional annotation projects.

Managerial Best Practices

Require Overlap

Mandate 10-20% minimum double-annotation on documents. You cannot measure agreement without overlap.

Continuous Measurement

Don't just measure once. Track IAA over time to detect drift and assess the impact of interventions.

Independent Assessment

"Not a focus group." Annotators must label independently to reveal true variance.

Questions Managers Must Ask Their Teams

"Show me the consistency of the training data."
"What specific measure are you using?"
"Why did you choose that metric?"
"What are you doing to improve low scores?"

Technical Debt Warning

Failing to measure IAA leads to inconsistent labels, which confuses models. This creates technical debt that is expensive to fix later. Prevention (via IAA) is cheaper than cure.
