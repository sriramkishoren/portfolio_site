Quantitative Data Types

Discrete

Countable data, typically integers. Finite, distinct values.

Examples
# of People # of Cars # of Products
AI Use: Countable outcomes

Plotted as discrete points

Continuous

Can take any value within a range (infinite possibilities).

Examples
Height Weight Time (ms)
AI Use: Regression

Why the Word "Regression"? The term originates from 19th-century statistician Francis Galton, who studied the "regression toward mediocrity". He observed that extreme data values (e.g., very tall parents) tended to "regress" or move back toward the average (mean) value in subsequent generations. While modern AI uses it for predictive modeling, the term stuck to describe how models map variables, often shrinking extreme predictions back toward the average.

Plotted as a line

Special Data Types

Binary

0|1

Yes/No, True/False, Success/Failure

Claude Shannon Legacy: Basis of all logic circuits (switches on/off) and AI evolution.

Interval

Numeric with meaningful differences but NO true zero (Arbitrary 0).

0°C / 32°F Not "absence" of heat
Weeks / Seasons Time Series
The "True Zero" Test

Does 0 mean "nothing"? NO.
Result: You cannot say 20°C is "twice as hot" as 10°C.

Ratio

Numeric with meaningful differences AND a true zero (Absolute 0).

Ratios are meaningful:

  • "Twice as tall"
  • "Half the weight"
  • Height, Weight, Age
The "True Zero" Test

Does 0 mean "nothing"? YES.
Result: 20 lbs IS "twice as heavy" as 10 lbs.

Scaling

Enabling "Apples-to-Apples" Comparisons

Standardization Z-Score

0
-1
+1

The "Fair Comparison" Problem:

Who performed better?
• You: 80% on a Hard Test (Class Avg: 70%)
• Friend: 85% on an Easy Test (Class Avg: 83%)

The Math (assuming Std Dev = 10):
• You: (80 - 70) ÷ 10 = +1.0 Z-Score
• Friend: (85 - 83) ÷ 10 = +0.2 Z-Score

Result: You win! (Your relative score is higher)

Goal: Center data around 0 with a standard deviation of 1.

When to use: When your data follows a Bell Curve. It enables algorithms to ignore the raw numbers (like 80 vs 85) and focus on the relative performance (distance from average).

Normalization Range 0 to 1

The Problem:

Student Rating
(Range 1-5)
Readiness Score
(Range 0-100)

Solution: By converting the "0-100 Readiness Score" into a "0-1" decimal, and doing the same for the "1-5 Student Rating," both variables end up on the exact same scale.

Why: It removes the "weight" of larger numbers so the algorithm treats variables objectively.

Algorithm Selection

You choose your algorithm based on your data type. (e.g., Classification for Discrete, Regression for Continuous)

Feature Engineering

Converting Qual to Quant data is the bridge that allows AI to function on real-world inputs.

Better Performance

"Squeezing the biggest gains" comes from labeling consistency and correct data handling, not just models.
