Krippendorff's Alpha (α)

The Gold Standard for Measuring Inter-Annotator Agreement

"I generally want to get a score over 0.8 before I go to the cost of developing a machine learning model. Otherwise, I don't feel that I have really consistent labeled data."

Based on insights from Dr. Ian McCullough, Johns Hopkins University

Why Krippendorff's Alpha?

It is the most robust statistic for assessing agreement because it handles the messy reality of data annotation projects where others fail.

  • Handles Missing Data: Annotators don't have to label every single document.
  • Variable Annotators: Works if Document A has 2 raters and Document B has 5.
  • Any Scale Type: Works for nominal (categories), ordinal (ranks), interval, and ratio data.
  • Corrects for Chance: Distinguishes true agreement from random guessing.

Technical Debt in AI

Low Agreement = Low Performance
Inconsistent training data teaches models contradictory patterns.
Managerial Insight: If your team isn't measuring Alpha, you are likely building "technical debt" into your model that will require costly retraining and relabeling later.

CRITICAL WARNING: The F-Measure Fallacy

Never use F-Measure for Inter-Annotator Agreement. F-Measure measures classifier performance (Machine vs. Truth), not annotator consistency (Human vs. Human). It is mathematically invalid for this purpose.

The Logic
α = 1 - Do / De

SUBSTITUTING VALUES

How to Improve Scores

Use "Ancestor" Categories

Instead of Honda vs Toyota (High Error), use Car. Reduces granularity but ensures reliable data.

Check Annotator Vitality

Formula: Vitality = α(total) - α(without_person)
If removing them increases Alpha, retrain or remove them.

Manager's Checklist

  • Require 10-20% overlap on all annotation tasks.
  • Ask team: "What metric are you using?" (Accept only Alpha/Kappa, reject F-measure).
  • Set a hard gate: No ML development until α ≥ 0.8.
  • Use Vitality scores to identify specific training needs (Recognition vs. Classification).
