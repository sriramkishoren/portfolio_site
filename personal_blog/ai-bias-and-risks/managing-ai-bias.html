SUPPORTING CONTEXT

Types of Bias in AI Systems

Before you can manage bias, you must recognize it. The ADAPT Awareness step begins here.

Human Cognitive Biases

Confirmation Bias

Seeking data that supports existing beliefs

Anchoring Bias

Over-relying on the first piece of information

Affinity Bias

Favoring people similar to us (Sam vs. Mo!)

Groupthink

Conforming to group opinion over independent analysis

Automation Bias

Over-trusting automated system outputs

Framing Effect

Different conclusions from same data presented differently

Data Biases

Sampling Bias

Sample not representative of target population

Historical Bias

Past inequities baked into training data

Reporting Bias

Only certain outcomes get recorded

Survivorship Bias

Analyzing only "survivors" of a process

Label Bias

Human annotator subjectivity in labeled data

Representation Bias

Underrepresentation of certain groups in data

Algorithmic & System Biases

Algorithmic Bias

Bias from model design and optimization choices

Feedback Loop Bias

Model outputs reinforce and amplify input patterns

Proxy Variable Bias

Neutral features encoding protected attributes (e.g., zip code ‚Üí race)

Deployment Bias

Model used in a context it wasn't designed for

SUPPORTING CONTEXT

Fairness Trade-offs: Why This Is So Hard

There is no single definition of "fairness." Organizations must choose which trade-offs they accept.

Demographic Parity

Equal positive prediction rates across groups

Equal Opportunity

Equal true positive rates across groups

Predictive Parity

Equal precision scores across groups

Calibration

Predicted probabilities match actual outcomes

The Impossibility Problem

These fairness definitions conflict with each other when data distributions differ across groups. It is mathematically impossible to satisfy all fairness criteria simultaneously. Organizations must decide which trade-offs they are comfortable accepting.

This is why you need a structured approach ‚Äî the ADAPT framework ‚Üì

The ADAPT Framework

A

AWARENESS

Step 1 of 5

Know and measure the different potential types of bias ‚Äî map them against protected classes and demographics you're concerned about.

SOURCE QUOTE

"In that loan example, there are four different types of bias. So measure all four and see how they match against potential protected classes or demographics that you're concerned about. Be aware of what the different potential biases are so that you can make an informed decision."

KEY ACTION

Create a bias inventory for your AI system ‚Äî identify and measure all relevant bias types before making any decisions.

üîó SAM vs. MO APPLICATION

Recognize that the evaluation contains Affinity Bias (likeability), Measurement Bias (social events as proxy), and Information Asymmetry (missing caregiving context).

D

DIVERSITY

Step 2 of 5

Contrast different metrics for consistency vs. divergence, and involve diverse viewpoints in your evaluation process.

SOURCE QUOTE

"Contrast the different metrics to observe whether they're providing consistent or divergent information. Is this something that is an anomaly or is this something that is a trend that you should be paying attention to? And involve other people and diverse viewpoints in looking at that decision as well."

KEY ACTION

Compare multiple metrics side by side and assemble diverse review teams ‚Äî including people with different backgrounds and lived experiences ‚Äî to evaluate findings.

üîó SAM vs. MO APPLICATION

Notice that performance scores and skills scores diverge ‚Äî Sam's likeability correlates with networking, not performance. A diverse review panel would flag this inconsistency.

A

ANALYSIS

Step 3 of 5

Holistically investigate the potential root causes ‚Äî ask whether there are variables or factors not being captured in the data.

SOURCE QUOTE

"Holistically investigate the potential reasons and cause ‚Äî like we did in the Mo versus Sam examples ‚Äî to say, hey, are there variables that I may not be capturing here? Are there factors here that I may not be considering?"

KEY ACTION

Conduct root-cause analysis ‚Äî go beyond surface data to uncover hidden context and confounding variables that shape the observed outcomes.

üîó SAM vs. MO APPLICATION

Investigate why Sam is "well liked" ‚Äî discover the Thirsty Thursdays networking events and Mo's daycare obligations. The hidden variable explains the entire apparent gap.

P

PUBLISH

Step 4 of 5

Publish your bias data and results to create transparency and explainability ‚Äî be open to criticism and alternative explanations.

SOURCE QUOTE

"You need to publish your biased data and results so you create a sense of transparency and explainability of the decision. You have to be open to criticism and alternative explanations that you may not have proposed. It fosters more of an open, transparent dialogue and helps people realize that you're about fairness and equity."

KEY ACTION

Create bias transparency reports, share findings openly with stakeholders, and actively invite criticism and alternative interpretations ‚Äî not as a courtesy, but as a process requirement.

üîó SAM vs. MO APPLICATION

Document and share the full analysis ‚Äî including the networking context ‚Äî with HR, leadership, and affected employees to build trust and enable informed challenges to the decision.

T

TRANSFORM

Step 5 of 5

Implement changes based on feedback, establish continuous learning loops, and improve the model over time. This is never a one-time fix.

SOURCE QUOTE

"You have to be prepared to implement changes that you have based on feedback and continuous learning and improve the model over time."

KEY ACTION

Implement changes based on findings, establish feedback loops, retrain models, update policies ‚Äî then cycle back to Awareness to repeat the process continuously.

üîó SAM vs. MO APPLICATION

Redesign the promotion evaluation process ‚Äî remove after-hours networking as an informal criterion, add structured skills assessments, and schedule regular review cycles to catch similar hidden biases.

ADAPT is a Continuous Cycle

Transform feeds directly back into Awareness. Bias management is not a one-time project ‚Äî it is an ongoing organizational commitment to fairness, transparency, and continuous learning.

KEY INSIGHTS

Six Things to Remember

01

Data alone doesn't tell the full story

Context is critical. Sam vs. Mo showed that identical performance scores hid completely different circumstances.

02

Most organizations don't dig deep enough

"Most organizations do not have or take the time to really get into the weeds and understand these differences."

03

Fairness trade-offs are real and unavoidable

The question isn't whether to accept trade-offs ‚Äî it's which trade-offs your organization is comfortable with.

04

AI makes existing bias measurable and visible

AI doesn't create new bias ‚Äî it surfaces bias that already existed. The question is what we do with that visibility.

05

Use ADAPT ‚Äî and repeat it continuously

Awareness ¬∑ Diversity ¬∑ Analysis ¬∑ Publish ¬∑ Transform ‚Üí back to Awareness. Never stop the cycle.

06

Transparency builds trust

Openness to criticism is not a weakness ‚Äî it is essential for building organizational trust in AI-driven decisions.
