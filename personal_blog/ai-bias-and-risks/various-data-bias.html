Biases in Collection

Collection Bias

Sampling Bias

Sample data is not representative of the population being analyzed.

‚ñº

Scenario

A tech company developing an AI-based facial recognition system for gender classification uses a dataset predominantly composed of images from public figures and celebrities sourced from the internet.

Bias Identified

Dataset likely underrepresents older individuals, people of varying attractiveness, and ethnic minorities. The AI may perform poorly for faces outside these demographic groups.

"You need to make sure that you have not just a large volume of training data, but that training data is diverse and balanced across different demographics ‚Äî or you might have differential performance."

Data Collection Bias

Bias introduced during the collection process itself.

‚ñº

Observer Bias

Data collectors unconsciously record data that confirms their expectations.

Instrument Bias

Faulty or poorly calibrated measurement tools produce systematic errors.

Selection Bias in Collection

Choosing convenient data sources over representative ones (e.g., health data only from hospitals).

Collection Bias

Survivorship Bias

Only looking at survivors from a process ‚Äî not those that did not make it through.

‚ñº

The WWII Scenario

During WWII, military analysts examined returning aircraft to find bullet damage. They saw damage concentrated on wings and fuselage, and proposed reinforcing these areas with additional armor.

The Flaw

The analysis only included planes that survived and returned. Missing from the data were planes shot down ‚Äî those likely hit in engines or cockpit, which never made it back.

The Solution

Armor was placed on the areas WITHOUT bullet holes ‚Äî engines and cockpit ‚Äî leading to more successful outcomes. The missing data told the real story.

Category 2

Biases in Reporting

Reporting

Reporting Bias

Only outcomes supporting a particular viewpoint are reported.

‚ñº

When only certain outcomes of data points are reported ‚Äî especially those that support a particular hypothesis or point of view ‚Äî the resulting dataset becomes inherently skewed.

Example

A pharmaceutical company publishes only clinical trials showing their drug is effective, shelving neutral or negative results. An AI trained on this data would dramatically overestimate drug effectiveness.

Reporting

Publication Bias

Only significant or positive results tend to get published.

‚ñº

Studies with significant or positive results are far more likely to be published, which skews the research landscape. Negative results sit unpublished in researchers' file drawers.

üìÅ The "File Drawer Problem"

Negative results sit unpublished ‚Üí AI systems trained on published literature develop a skewed view of what interventions actually work.

Social Desirability Bias

Respondents answer how they think the data collector wants to hear, not what they truly believe.

‚ñº

Scenario

A tech company builds a sentiment analysis AI using survey data on sensitive topics like racial discrimination and political views. Respondents provide socially acceptable answers ‚Äî not their true opinions ‚Äî to avoid judgment or backlash.

‚ö† Implication

The AI produces inaccurate sentiment analyses, failing to reflect the true sentiment of the population it's meant to understand.

"People are going to report what they think is the right answer as opposed to what they truly believe ‚Äî and AI models inherit this distortion."

‚Äî Dr. Ian McCullough, JHU

Category 3

Systematic Biases

Systematic Bias

Historical Bias

Biased results present in historical data are perpetuated in future models.

‚ñº

Scenario

A company develops a hiring algorithm to screen resumes and predict job performance based on historical hiring data. The training data predominantly includes resumes of employees who were hired and performed well ‚Äî reflecting historical biases favoring certain demographics.

Mitigation Insight (from source)

"It might be better to go back and think about what are the qualifications we want ‚Äî and identify those qualities in applicants independently."

AI masking of demographic indicators is one approach, but may not fully solve the problem if resumes are still evaluated against historical patterns.

Systematic Bias

Algorithmic Bias

Bias arising from the design and function of the algorithm itself.

‚ñº

Scenario

An AI system predicts the likelihood of patients developing complications after surgery using preoperative health data. The model uses data where certain demographic groups are overrepresented, and where missing data is asymmetric across protected and non-protected groups.

Core Problem

Label removal is not the same across protected and non-protected groups. This asymmetric missing data enters the model, causing the algorithm to underpredict risk for older patients or those with complex medical histories.

‚ö† Implication

The AI system may fail to adequately predict complications for diverse patient groups, risking patient safety and leading to suboptimal medical care for the most vulnerable patients.

Analytical Framework

Bias in Homogeneous vs. Heterogeneous Data

Homogeneous Data

Non-Representative Sample

The sample does not represent the broader population ‚Äî you're only seeing a narrow slice of reality.

Overfitting

Models learn to perform very well on the narrow training data but fail to generalize to more diverse real-world data.

Confirmation Bias

Reinforces pre-existing assumptions and leads to biased conclusions because you haven't examined a broader sample.

"You'll be really good at prediction on that sample of data, but it's consistently wrong in that it doesn't represent the general population."

Heterogeneous Data

Measurement Bias

If data is collected differently from different groups, this leads to variability in data quality across populations.

Complex Interaction Effects

Different subgroups have different characteristics and behaviors, creating interaction effects between variables.

Confounding Variables

You can't determine if it's variable A or variable B making the difference because you don't see contrast between them.

üí° The Dimmer Switch Analogy

"You turn the dimmer switch up, the light gets bright. You turn it down, the light gets lower. But if the circuit breaker is off, it doesn't matter how you turn that dimmer switch ‚Äî the light's not getting brighter or darker. It's just off." Variables interact in ways that completely override individual effects.

"It's always good to have the data. Whether you want to use the data in a model outcome is another story, but you can keep some variables to simply test if there is bias ‚Äî either legal, systematic, or data bias ‚Äî present in your solution."

‚Äî Dr. Ian McCullough, Johns Hopkins University

Practical Resolution

Mitigating Bias

Seven Core Strategies ‚Äî Dr. Ian McCullough, JHU

1

Representative Sampling

Ensure the sample is representative and randomly selected to avoid systematic exclusion of any group.

2

Validated Measurement Instruments

"Try not to make up your own survey instruments. Try and use things that people have already done and tested before." ‚Äî Dr. McCullough

3

Train Data Collectors

Thoroughly train data collectors to minimize observer bias and standardize collection methodology across all groups.

4

Multiple Sources & Contexts

Collect data from multiple diverse sources and contexts to reduce the influence of any single biased source.

5

Transparent Reporting

Transparently report ALL data results ‚Äî including null and negative results ‚Äî to prevent publication and reporting bias.

6

Regular Auditing

Regularly audit and evaluate both data and algorithms for bias, especially as models are updated and deployed in new contexts.

7

Diverse Perspectives

Include diverse perspectives in the data collection and analysis process to surface blind spots that homogeneous teams may miss.
