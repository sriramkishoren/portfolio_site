The Human Bias Landscape

Both human and machine biases can significantly affect decision-making and outcomes in AI systems. While human bias is often deeply ingrained (cognitive shortcuts), machine bias can arise from the data and algorithms used in AI, leading to potentially harmful consequences.

Confirmation Bias

Seeking information that supports existing beliefs while ignoring contradictory evidence.

Example: Only reading news that aligns with your politics.

Anchoring Bias

Over-relying on the first piece of information offered (the "anchor") when making decisions.

Example: Judging a salary offer based solely on the previous salary.

Availability Heuristic

Judging the likelihood of an event based on how easily examples come to mind.

Example: Fearing plane crashes more than car accidents due to news.

Implicit Bias

Unconscious attitudes or stereotypes that affect our understanding, actions, and decisions.

Example: Associating certain professions with specific genders.

Status Quo Bias

Preference for the current state of affairs; change is perceived as a loss.

Example: Keeping default insurance plans even if better ones exist.

Framing Effect

Drawing different conclusions from the same information depending on how it is presented.

Example: "90% survival rate" vs. "10% mortality rate".

Machine Bias Taxonomy

Historical Bias

Training data reflects past societal inequities (e.g., hiring data from an era of discrimination).

Representation Bias

Training data under-represents certain groups (e.g., facial recognition trained mostly on light skin).

Measurement Bias

Choosing features/labels that are poor proxies for reality (e.g., using "arrests" as a proxy for "crime").

Aggregation Bias

Treating heterogeneous groups as homogeneous, masking subgroup differences.

Feedback Loop

Model outputs influence future data collection, amplifying original biases.

Deployment Bias

Using a model in a context or population different from where it was trained.

Real-World Case Studies

Simpson's Paradox (Berkeley Admissions)

When aggregate data contradicts subgroup data.

The Situation

In the 1970s, UC Berkeley was sued for gender bias. The university admitted women at a lower rate overall than men. However, when looking at individual departments, most actually admitted women at higher rates than men.

Key Insight

Simpson's Paradox illustrates how data can appear to show one trend when viewed in aggregate, but a different trend when analyzed in subgroups. This paradox highlights the importance of context in understanding bias, particularly in situations like gender discrimination claims.

Healthcare Screening Bias

When "blindness" to race causes harm.

An algorithm used cost as a proxy for health needs. Because Black patients had less spent on them historically (due to systemic access issues), the model predicted they were "healthier" than equally sick White patients.

  • Black patients have 26.3% more chronic illnesses.
  • Comparison: 4.8 conditions (Black) vs 3.8 conditions (White) at same risk score.

The Consequence of Removing Context

Removing the "bias" (making the algorithm race-blind without adjusting for the cost discrepancy) effectively denied screening to sicker Black patients.

Predictive Policing & Feedback Loops

Arrest data is not the same as crime data.

Police algorithms often use "arrests" as a proxy for "crime." However, arrests are heavily biased by where police are deployed. Drug overdoses (a closer ground truth) were geographically distinct from drug arrests.

The Loop: Police go to Area A → Make arrests → Data says "Area A has high crime" → Algorithm sends more police to Area A → More arrests.

Critical Nuance

Predictive policing that does not consider racial disparities in crime rates offers an inaccurate view of effectiveness. Implications regarding factors like racial disparities and data usage must be central to the analysis.

The Human Baseline Framework

"Don't compare AI to perfection — compare it to the status quo."

Human (Status Quo)

Inconsistent, fatigued, biases are hard-wired, hard to audit.

Error Rate: Variable

AI Solution

Consistent, biases can be measured and engineered out.

Potentially Lower Error

"In some cases, it may be unethical to deny people the AI solution just because it isn't perfect, if it's better than the human alternative."

Fairness is Not One-Size-Fits-All

Demographic Parity

Acceptance rates must be equal across all groups (e.g., 50% men, 50% women accepted).

Equalized Odds

True Positive rates and False Positive rates should be equal across groups.

Calibration

If the model predicts 70% risk, it should be correct 70% of the time for all groups.

Individual Fairness

Similar individuals should receive similar outcomes, regardless of group.

The Impossibility Theorem

Mathematically, you often cannot satisfy all fairness definitions simultaneously. You must choose which definition matters most for your specific context.

Mitigation Strategies

Phase 1: Pre-Processing
  • Diverse Data Collection
  • Re-sampling / Reweighting

Phase 2: In-Processing
  • Fairness Constraints in Loss Function

  • Threshold Adjustment

Key Takeaways

1

Bias is not always "bad." Sometimes removing it (like in healthcare) causes more harm than leaving it in. Context is king.

2

Feedback Loops are dangerous. When model outputs become model inputs (like in policing), bias is amplified rapidly.

3

Human Baseline. Don't let the fear of imperfect AI prevent improvements over the flawed human status quo.

4

Fairness requires trade-offs. You cannot satisfy every definition of fairness at the same time.
