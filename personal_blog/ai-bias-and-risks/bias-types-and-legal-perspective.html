Understanding Bias Definitions, Legal Frameworks, Data Pitfalls, and the Mathematics of Fairness in AI Systems

Foundation

Two Definitions, One Word

Bias carries both an ethical and a mathematical meaning — and both matter in AI.

Ethical / Cultural

Prejudice in favor of or against a thing, person, or group compared with another, usually in a way that's unfair.

Mathematical / Statistical

A systematic distortion of a statistical result due to a factor not allowed for in its derivation.

These two definitions are related but different — and together they shape our understanding of bias in AI.

Humans have 50+ documented cognitive biases. We shouldn't expect it to be different in machines.

Human Bias

Human Cognitive Bias Landscape

"Humans are not without bias. We must understand that. Within that context, let us look at machines."

Bandwagon Effect

Following along with what everybody else is doing, regardless of evidence.

Example: Adopting popular opinions without personal research

Placebo Effect

Experiencing different outcomes simply because you believe you are in a study.

Example: Patients improve when told they received treatment

Confirmation Bias

Seeking information that confirms existing beliefs while ignoring contradictory evidence.

Example: Only reading news sources aligned with your views

Anchoring Bias

Over-relying on the first piece of information received when making decisions.

Example: Salary negotiations anchored by the first number offered

Availability Heuristic

Judging the likelihood of events based on how easily examples come to mind.

Example: Fearing plane crashes after seeing news coverage

Implicit / Unconscious Bias

Automatic stereotyping that occurs outside of conscious awareness.

Example: Making assumptions about competence based on appearance

Groupthink

Conforming to group opinion over independent analysis to maintain harmony.

Example: Teams suppressing dissent to avoid conflict

Status Quo Bias

Preference for the current state of affairs, resisting change even when beneficial.

Example: Keeping default options even when better ones exist

Dunning-Kruger Effect

Overestimating one's competence in areas where knowledge is limited.

Example: Novice investors overconfident in stock-picking ability

Framing Effect

Drawing different conclusions from the same data depending on how it is presented.

Example: "90% survival rate" vs. "10% mortality rate"

Legal Framework

Legal Perspective of Bias

Key legal definitions that govern fairness in algorithmic decision-making.

Protected Class

A category where bias is relevant and likely to differentially affect certain groups.

Race Religion Gender Sexual Orientation

Sensitive Characteristics

Features or attributes where algorithmic decisions may introduce bias.

Example: Race in healthcare allows more accurate screening of chronic illness

Disparate Treatment

Occurs when individuals are treated differently based on protected characteristics.

Legal Definition

Disparate Outcome / Demographic Parity

When outcomes differ across groups regardless of intention.

Legal Definition

Equal Opportunity (Process)

Ensuring that all individuals have the same chance of a favorable outcome.

Legal Definition

Equal Accuracy

Striving for overall accuracy to be consistent across different groups.

Legal Definition

Calibration

Achieving equal accuracy at each score level within a predictive model.

Legal Definition

Predictive Parity

Ensuring equal accuracy at critical decision points within a model.

Legal Definition

Key Insight: These legal definitions were derived from decades or centuries of bias in human beings — they may or may not exist in AI. AI is making existing bias measurable, observable, and visible.

Additional Legal Concepts

Four-Fifths Rule (80% Rule)

A selection rate for any group less than 80% of the highest group's rate is evidence of adverse impact.

Proxy Discrimination

When neutral criteria serve as stand-ins for protected characteristics (e.g., zip code as proxy for race).

Intentional vs. Unintentional

Legal frameworks address both deliberate bias and systemic disparate impact.

Data Perspective

Data Perspective of Bias

Legal perspective defines what is protected. Data perspective shows where bias enters.

Source-Material Bias Types

Sampling Bias

Sample data is not representative of the population being analyzed.

Example: Chicago gun violence data may not generalize to other regions

High

Reporting Bias

Only certain outcomes or data points are reported, skewing the dataset.

Example: Police shootings reported; non-shooting altercations may not be

High

Social Desirability Bias

Respondents provide socially acceptable answers rather than the truth.

Example: Survey participants underreporting unhealthy behaviors

Medium

Historical Bias

Biases in historical data are perpetuated in current models via feedback loops.

Example: Predictive policing — more arrests → more policing → more arrests

Critical

Algorithmic Bias

Bias introduced by the design and functioning of the algorithm itself.

High

Survivorship Bias

Focusing on data from "survivors" while ignoring those that didn't make it through.

Example: Only examining consumer returns, not products that never reached market

Medium

Data Collection Bias

Bias from inconsistent or flawed data collection methodologies.

Medium

Publication Bias

Studies with positive results are more likely to be published, skewing perception.

Medium

Trade-off: Appears fair but produces unequal accuracy and profit across groups

With no constraints, the algorithm sets different thresholds for each population to maximize total profit. The blue group gets a higher credit score requirement, while the orange group threshold may be adjusted independently.

Trade-off: Maximizes revenue but results in different standards for different populations — potential disparate treatment

The threshold is set so the same fraction of blue and orange applicants receive loans. Since the orange population has more low-credit-score applicants, this means fewer qualified blue applicants get loans to create equity with the orange population.

Trade-off: Hurts qualified individuals in the blue group to achieve equal approval rates

The same fraction of people who CAN pay back actually get loans in both groups (equal true positive rate). However, you don't know who will actually pay back in advance — making this criterion impossible to optimize directly at decision time.

Trade-off: Theoretically fair but practically impossible to implement without ground-truth knowledge

"It is impossible to create a uniformly fair strategy when data is heterogeneous. These strategies conflict. Trade-offs must be assessed: which bias are you comfortable with?"

— The Impossibility Theorem demonstrated empirically

Fairness

Fairness Definitions & the Impossibility Theorem

Multiple definitions of fairness exist — and they cannot all be satisfied simultaneously.

Demographic Parity

Equal positive prediction rates across all groups, regardless of actual outcome.

Equalized Odds

Equal true positive and false positive rates across groups.

Calibration

Predicted probabilities match actual outcomes for each group.

Individual Fairness

Similar individuals receive similar outcomes, regardless of group membership.

Predictive Parity

Equal precision (positive predictive value) across all groups at critical decision points.

The Impossibility Theorem

You cannot satisfy all fairness criteria simultaneously when base rates differ between groups.

Chouldechova (2017) • Kleinberg, Mullainathan & Raghavan (2016)

Stakeholders must choose which fairness definition matters most for their specific use case.

Key Insight

AI as Bias Illuminator

Dr. McCullough's central thesis — AI reveals bias rather than creating it.

"AI is not necessarily increasing bias or introducing bias as much as it's making the bias that exists measurable, observable, and visible — and it allows us to do something about it."

Before AI

Bias exists but is invisible, unmeasurable, and embedded in human decisions. Legal definitions were derived from centuries of documented human bias — but detection was inconsistent and after-the-fact.

With AI

Bias becomes quantifiable, auditable, and actionable. Algorithms make bias patterns visible in data, enabling measurement across protected classes and systematic mitigation.

Healthcare Example: Including race as a sensitive characteristic allows the model to more accurately screen health outcomes. Removing it can lead to under-selection of at-risk patients — sometimes including sensitive variables produces more equitable outcomes.

The question is not whether AI has bias, but what we do about it once it becomes visible.

Loan Applications

Source

Four fairness strategies all produce different trade-offs with heterogeneous data.

The case study demonstrates that Group Unaware, Max Profit, Demographic Parity, and Equal Opportunity strategies each create winners and losers when the blue and orange populations have different credit score distributions. The blue population loses $700 while the orange profits $20,300 under Group Unaware — revealing how "neutral" policies produce disparate impact.

Lesson: Uniform fairness is impossible with heterogeneous data

Healthcare Screening

Source

Including race as a sensitive characteristic improves screening accuracy.

Retaining race as a variable allows the model to account for statistically different rates of chronic illness between populations. Removing it would cause under-selection of at-risk patients — effectively denying them health services. This demonstrates that bias removal can cause disparate impact when data is genuinely heterogeneous.

Lesson: Sometimes including sensitive variables produces more equitable outcomes

Predictive Policing

Source

Historical bias creates self-reinforcing feedback loops in arrest data.

Arrest data does not equal ground truth for crime. More policing in an area → more arrests → model sees more "crime" → even more policing. Historical biases are perpetuated and amplified through this feedback loop.

Lesson: Feedback loops amplify and entrench existing inequities

Takeaways

Key Takeaways

1

Bias has two definitions — ethical and mathematical — both matter in AI and both shape how we evaluate fairness.

2

Legal frameworks define what is protected; data science reveals where bias enters — two perspectives that must work together.

3

AI is not creating new bias — it is making existing bias measurable and visible, enabling us to address it systematically.

4

It is mathematically impossible to satisfy all fairness criteria simultaneously when data distributions differ between groups.

5

Including sensitive variables can sometimes produce more equitable outcomes than excluding them — as shown in healthcare screening.

6

Trade-offs are unavoidable — the question is which bias you are comfortable with, and that requires deliberate stakeholder discussion.

7

Responsible AI requires ongoing monitoring, not one-time fixes — bias can emerge, drift, and amplify through feedback loops.
